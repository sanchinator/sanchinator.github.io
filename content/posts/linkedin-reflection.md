I didn't have LinkedIn before this academic year. No account, no profile, nothing. I had a GitHub and figured that was enough. Writing posts about yourself on a professional platform felt performative, and I didn't really see who I'd be performing for.

The LinkedIn workshop with Patrick Van Renterghem in October is what got me to actually make one. He made a pretty blunt point: recruiters Google you before they meet you. If they find nothing, that's not neutral - it's a gap. I'd rather control what shows up.

Setting up the profile took longer than expected. The headline alone went through a few versions before I landed on "Cyber Security 3rd year Student | Aspiring Cybersecurity Professional." Generic? Maybe. But it says more than just "student at Howest." I added a banner, wrote the about section, listed my education, my CEH certification, the international project I did in Katowice, and eventually my internship at CM in Brussels.

Then came the part I was dreading: posting. My first real one I was proud of was about passing the CEH v13 exam.

After that it got easier. I wrote about the Tech & Meet sessions I attended - threat intelligence with CCB, the DeepSeek talk, .NET 10, Belgium's cyber defence setup.

I'm at 140 connections now. Mostly classmates, a few speakers from events, some people from the cybersecurity community in Belgium. Not huge, but I actually know who all of them are.

The thing I didn't expect: LinkedIn turned out to be useful not because of reach or impressions, but because it forces you to put into words what you actually did and what you thought about it. That's the same muscle you need for job interviews, and I'd rather practice it now than figure it out under pressure.
